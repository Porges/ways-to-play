<Pronounce noun="true" lang="ja-Latn"  file="pronunciation_ja_八八.mp3" pronouncer="mezashi">Hachi-Hachi</Pronounce> (<span lang="ja">八八</span>, ‘88’) is the preëminent [<span class="noun" lang="ja-Latn">Hanafuda</span>](articles/cards/japan/hanafuda/hanafuda.md) gambling game for 3–7 players. In many old English sources it is considered synonymous with ‘<span class="noun" lang="ja-Latn">hanafuda</span>’ or ‘<span class="noun" lang="ja-Latn">hana-awase</span>’ and isn’t given a more specific name.

The name derives from the objective of the game; the standard version of the game is for three active players, and there are 264 total card points available, so the goal for each player is to obtain at least their share, i.e. 264 ÷ 3 = **88** points.

Despite being — historically, at least — the quintessential[^prem] <span class="noun" lang="ja-Latn">Hanafuda</span> game, it is also one of the most difficult. The play of the cards is the same as in other <span class="noun" lang="ja-Latn">Hanafuda</span> games, but the procedures of betting and payment combine to increase the complexity. There are also multiple sets of <span lang="ja-Latn">yaku</span>, and specialized equipment that was commonly used with the game.

[^prem]: _ichiban_?

Because of this, I present a simplified base game first, and each subsequent section introduces another extension of the rules until the full version of the game is attained.[^zds] As a game that was often played in secret for money, there is no one ‘true’ set of rules, and there are also many optional variations and regional rules differences.

[^zds]: This approach was inspired by @CurriculumOfVira.

## The Rules

The full game of 88 has many complications, but we can identify subsets of the game that are independent of each other. Each additional topic in the following should identify a “complete” game that is playable without adding any of the rules that follow it. They are listed hopefully in some vague notion of increasing “arbitrariness” and complexity.

### 0: Core Rules

The base game is for **3 players**. <span class="noun" lang="ja-Latn">Hachi-Hachi</span> is played with a standard [<span class="noun" lang="ja-Latn">Hanafuda</span>](articles/cards/japan/hanafuda/hanafuda.md) deck of 48 cards.

#### Setup

Determine the dealer for the first round. In subsequent rounds, the winner of the previous round is the dealer.

Some suggested methods to determine the first dealer are:[@HachiHachi 12]

- deal one card each and the player who receives the card nearest to the start of the year is the dealer (in case of tie, redeal for only the tied players)
- deal cards to each player until the Crane/Pine Bright is dealt — the recipient is the dealer
- deal two cards each and the player with the highest point value of cards is the dealer (in case of tie, redeal for only the tied players)

The dealer shuffles and deals 6 cards to the centre of the table (the **field**, <span lang="ja">場</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">ba</span>), then 7 cards to each player (anti-clockwise) by dealing four cards each followed by three cards each. The remainder of the deck becomes the **draw pile** (<span lang="ja">めくり札</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">mekuri-fuda</span>[@HachiHachi 15] ‘turning cards’ or <span lang="ja">山札</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">yama-fuda</span>[@HanafudaNoKihon 57] ‘mountain cards’) and is placed face-down next to the field.

If all 4 cards of a single month are dealt to the field, this is a misdeal; shuffle and deal again.

#### Play

The dealer plays first, and turns pass anti-clockwise. On each turn, a player:

1. **Plays a card from their hand** to the field. If it matches the month of a card in the field, both cards are captured and placed (face-up) in front of the player, forming a score pile. If the played card matches two cards, the player chooses which to capture. If it matches three cards, all three are captured. If it matches nothing, the card remains in the field.

2. **Draws the top card** from the draw pile and plays it to the field in the same manner.

Each player's captured cards should be kept face-up and arranged by type (Brights, Animals, Ribbons, Plain) so that all players can see them.

Play continues until all cards have been played or a <span lang="ja-Latn">deki-yaku</span> (below) has been claimed.

#### Scoring

The values of the cards in the deck are as follows:

| Card Type                          | Points | Cards |
| ---------------------------------- | ------ | ----- |
| Bright (<span lang="ja">光</span>)  | 20     | 5     |
| Animal (<span lang="ja">種</span>)  | 10     | 9     |
| Ribbon (<span lang="ja">短冊</span>) | 5      | 10    |
| Plain (<span lang="ja">カス</span>)  | 1      | 24    |

Thus the total point value of the deck is **264 points**. The aim of each player is to capture at least one third of this value, or **88 points** worth of cards.

At the end of the round, each player scores the difference between their captured card points and 88. Payments of points are made from those player(s) who did not achieve their par score to those who did. The game is zero-sum as it was originally played for money or money’s worth. (If one player makes their par, they will be paid by the two other players; if two players make their par, each will be paid by the third player.)

### 1: <span lang="ja-Latn">Deki-yaku</span> · Captured Combinations

Next we add in <span lang="ja-Latn">deki-yaku</span> (<span lang="ja">出来役</span>, ‘result combinations’), sometimes referred to as <span lang="ja-Latn">ba-yaku</span> (‘field combinations’).[@HachiHachi 66] These are valuable combinations of cards which can be captured during play.

Scoring for <span lang="ja-Latn">yaku</span> is given in units of <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span> (<span lang="ja">貫</span>). Traditionally, 1 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span> = 12 points, although modern rulesets often use 1 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span> = 10 points.

If you complete a <span lang="ja-Latn">deki-yaku</span>, at the end of your turn play stops and you are paid the <span lang="ja-Latn">yaku</span> value by **each** other player. No payments are subsequently made for card points.

**Note**: Scores for <span lang="ja-Latn">yaku</span> vary depending on the ruleset (I have collected over 50 rulesets and have yet to find two the same except where one is clearly derived from the other), so the values following should be considered “typical”. They match current commercially-produced rules printed by Nintendo.

<dl>

<dt>Red Poetry Ribbons · <span lang="ja">赤短</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">aka-tan</span> · 7 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span></dt>
<dd>The three red <span lang="ja-Latn">tanzaku</span> with writing (Pine, Plum, Cherry). The red <span lang="ja-Latn">tanzaku</span> without writing do not count.</dd>

<dt>Blue Ribbons · <span lang="ja">青短</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">ao-tan</span> · 7 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span></dt>
<dd>The three blue <span lang="ja-Latn">tanzaku</span> (Peony, Chrysanthemum, Maple).</dd>

<dt>Four Brights · <span lang="ja">四光</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">shi-kō</span> · 10 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span></dt>
<dd>Four Bright cards (excluding the Rain Man).</dd>

<dt>Five Brights · <span lang="ja">五光</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">go-kō</span> · 12 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span></dt>
<dd>All five Bright cards.[^impossible]</dd>

### 2: <span lang="ja-Latn">Sage</span> · Continuing

If during play you complete a <span lang="ja-Latn">deki-yaku</span> but think you can score another (or improve four brights to five), instead of stopping the round you can call ‘continue’ (<span lang="ja">下げ</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">sage</span>).

If you manage to complete an additional <span lang="ja-Latn">yaku</span>, you score for **both** at the end. (Do not score for both four and five brights; only the higher one counts.)

A player can **cancel** their <span lang="ja-Latn">sage</span> on their turn before playing any cards (e.g. if other players threaten to make a <span lang="ja-Latn">deki-yaku</span>), but they only win **half** the value of their original <span lang="ja-Latn">yaku</span>. This is also the case if the cards run out without any further <span lang="ja-Latn">yaku</span> being formed.


### 3: Round-End <span lang="ja-Latn">Deki-yaku</span>

If by the end of the round no one has captured any <span lang="ja-Latn">deki-yaku</span>, check if anyone has achieved one of the following.[^roundend] If so, they win that score from **each** other player (and again, no payments are made for card points):

[^roundend]: Note that some rulesets treat some of these as _round-ending_ <span lang="ja-Latn">yaku</span>, the same as the others.

<dl>

<dt>Sixteen Plain · <span lang="ja">素十六</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">su-jūroku</span> · 12 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span></dt>
<dd>A player captures 16 or more Plain cards; they win. Score an additional <strong>2 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span></strong> for each card over 16. (For this purpose, all Willow cards count as Plain.)</dd>

<dt>Double Eights · <span lang="ja">二八</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">futahachi</span> · 10 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span></dt>
<dd>A player captures 168 or more card points (88 + 80); they win. Score an additional <strong>1 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span></strong> for each point over 168.</dd>

<dt>All Eights · <span lang="ja">総八</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">sōhachi</span> · 10 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span></dt>
<dd>All three players capture exactly 88 card points. The dealer wins.</dd>

</dl>

### 4: <span lang="ja-Latn">Te-yaku</span> · Hand Combinations

<span lang="ja-Latn">Te-yaku</span> (<span lang="ja">手役</span>, ‘hand combinations’)[^tsukiyaku] are dealt hands that are rare but are usually poor for capturing cards. As compensation, players holding them are paid by the other players at the start of the round.

[^tsukiyaku]: Rarely also called <span lang="ja">付き役</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">tsuki-yaku</span>, ‘luck combinations’.[@HanaAwase 10] Note that this is homophonous with ‘moon combinations’ and the source doesn’t actually give the kanji, but I believe this is correct because of the context.

> [!aside]
>
> Many traditional rulesets give the list of <span lang="ja-Latn">te&shy;yaku</span> in one group, with all possible combinations and their scores listed. When presented in this way there are seemingly 53 <span lang="ja-Latn">te&shy;yaku</span>! It is much simpler to remember two smaller sets of <span lang="ja-Latn">yaku</span> and to recognize that when scoring for two <span lang="ja-Latn">yaku</span>, the total score is simply the sum of each individual score.
>
> However, in some extremely rare cases, I have seen rulesets where the scoring is not a simple linear combination. In this case, recourse to a lookup chart is required.

There are two categories of <span lang="ja-Latn">te-yaku</span>: those based upon having many Plain cards and those based upon Poker-like combinations. A player can claim **one from each category** (choose the highest-scoring in each).

#### <span lang="ja-Latn">Te-yaku</span> based on sets

These hands are poor in play because having multiple cards of the same month limits what you can capture. For example, with three-of-a-kind, those three cards can only capture one more card total.

A player claiming one of these <span lang="ja-Latn">te-yaku</span> must expose the relevant cards before the round is played.[^exposing] This involves placing the cards which form the combination on the table. Lone cards are not exposed except for the one involved in the One-Two-Four <span lang="ja-Latn">yaku</span>.

[^exposing]: But see below for variations on exposure requirements.

If a player is dealt a bad hand but manages to improve it, they can be rewarded:

### 6: Playing with More Than 3 Players

With 4–6 players, only 3 will play each round. At the start, players decide whether to play or drop out.

#### Dropout Phase

After dealing but before revealing <span lang="ja-Latn">te-yaku</span>, in turn order from the dealer going anti-clockwise, each player may choose to drop out (<span lang="ja">降り</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">ori</span>, ‘give up’) if they have a poor hand. However, they must pay a **dropout penalty** (<span lang="ja">逃げ料</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">nige-ryō</span> or <span lang="ja">下り賃</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">orichin</span>,[@TrumpHanafuda 188] ‘escape fee’):

- The dealer (1st seat) pays **1 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span>**
- 2nd seat pays **1½ <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span>**
- 3rd seat pays **2 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span>**
- And so on, increasing by ½ <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span> per seat

This is the simplified modern version — see below for the more complicated traditional calculation.

Cards from inactive players are collected and shuffled into the draw pile. The dropout penalties are paid into a pot which is taken by the winner of the round.

If fewer than 3 players wish to play:
- **2 players**: They may agree to split the pot and skip the round, or play a 2-player game (see below).
- **1 player**: They automatically win and collect all already-paid penalties. Note that if this happens it is usually held that this does not count as one of the 12 hands in a full ‘year’ game (see below), and the winner does not contribute a black chip towards the bank.[@HanaAwase 46]

#### With 7 Players

Shuffle a blank card (included in many <span class="noun" lang="ja-Latn">Hanafuda</span> decks) into the deck. Whoever is dealt the blank card drops out immediately — their remaining 6 cards become the field. The blank card plays no further role. The other 6 players then proceed with the normal dropout phase.

### 7: Buying Out Players

When playing with more than three, when the third player commits to play, any remaining players who were forced to sit out may need to be **bought out** if they have valuable hands:

- They are paid **half** the value of their <span lang="ja-Latn">te-yaku</span> by each of the 3 active players.
- They are paid **6 points** (or 3 points?[@HanaAwase 39]) by each active player for each card that could contribute to a <span lang="ja-Latn">deki-yaku</span>. There are 11 cards that count here (termed <span lang="ja">役物</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">yaku-mono</span>[@HanaAwase 39] ‘<span lang="ja-Latn">yaku</span> things’): the three red poetry ribbons, three blue ribbons, and five brights.

Players are not paid for any complete <span lang="ja-Latn">deki-yaku</span> that they might have been dealt in their hand.

### 8: Playing with 2 Players · <span lang="ja">差し</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">sashi</span>

If all but two players drop out, they may elect to play against each other. The entire deck will not be exhausted (14 cards will remain), so forming <span lang="ja-Latn">deki-yaku</span> is harder and luck plays a larger role.

When calculating card points, instead of subtracting 88, the players’ scores are subtracted from each other and the difference is paid to the winner. The maximum payment is limited to **5 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span>**.[@HachiHachi 83]

The two players may instead negotiate to skip the round and split the dropout penalties instead. If one refuses, they must play.

### 9: Responsibility

Because it is possible to sabotage one player by gifting another an easy win, **responsibility** rules exist:

If a player has captured all but one card needed for a <span lang="ja-Latn">deki-yaku</span>, any player holding the last card **may not discard it** until their final turn. If they do discard it earlier and the first player captures it, the discarding player is **responsible** for the entire payment that the third player would normally owe.

### 10: Field Multipliers [Optional]

> [!aside]
>
> <span class="noun" lang="ja-Latn">Hachi-Hachi</span> sets include a <span lang="ja-Latn">gunbai</span> (war fan) token to indicate when the round is doubled or quadrupled.

Before play begins, count the Bright cards in the field to determine a **multiplier** that applies to all payments for the round:

<dl>

<dt>Small Field · <span lang="ja">小場</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">koba</span></dt>
<dd>No Brights in the field. Payments are normal (×1).</dd>

<dt>Large Field · <span lang="ja">大場</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">ōba</span></dt>
<dd>The Crane (Pine), Curtain (Cherry), or Moon (Miscanthus) is in the field. All payments are <strong>doubled</strong> (×2).</dd>

<dt>Grand Field · <span lang="ja">絶場</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">zetsuba</span></dt>
<dd>The Rain Man (Willow) or Phoenix (Paulownia) is in the field. All payments are <strong>quadrupled</strong> (×4).</dd>

</dl>

**Optional:** If multiple Brights are in the field, apply the largest multiplier this round and carry the others forward to subsequent rounds.

### 11: Going in Blind · <span lang="ja-Latn">Mizuten</span> [Optional]

> [!aside]
>
> <span class="noun" lang="ja-Latn">Hachi-Hachi</span> sets include a <span lang="ja-Latn">mizuten</span> token to track when the dealer has declared this.

As a gamble, the dealer may declare <span lang="ja-Latn">mizuten</span> (<span lang="ja">不見転</span>, ‘not seeing’) and commit to playing **without looking at their cards**. They cannot drop out.

If they score at least 89 card points, they win **1 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span>** from each other player. Optionally, if they fail, they pay 1 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span> to each other player.

### 12: Scoring with Counters

Since the game is zero-sum, players traditionally use tokens similar to Go stones to keep score:
- **Black stones** = 1 point
- **White stones** = 1 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span> (12 points)

Players typically start with 5 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span>.

If a player runs out of counters, they may borrow in units of 5 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span> from the bank. Each loan is indicated by a red **debt token** and must be repaid at game end.

### 13: The Campaign Game and <span lang="ja-Latn">Ginmi</span>

A full game of <span class="noun" lang="ja-Latn">Hachi-Hachi</span> consists of **12 rounds** (one “year”).

> [!aside]
>
> <span class="noun" lang="ja-Latn">Hachi-Hachi</span> sets include a device (often the box lid) with 12 holes to place these bank payments in.

After each round, the winner pays **1 point** to the bank. These 12 points track the rounds played and are given to the player with the **fewest** points at the end of the year.

> [!aside]
>
> <span class="noun" lang="ja-Latn">Hachi-Hachi</span> sets include a <span lang="ja-Latn">ginmi</span> medal token to track when a player has won 2 consecutive rounds.

The player with the most points at any time is called <span lang="ja-Latn">ginmi</span> (<span lang="ja">吟味</span>). If a player remains <span lang="ja-Latn">ginmi</span> for **3 rounds in a row**, they receive a **10 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span> bonus** from each other player.

#### Binding

In the final round of the year, to prevent players from strategically dropping out to protect a lead, the round may be declared **binding** — no player may drop out.

## Variant Rules

### Target 80

It is possible to use a target of 80 points instead of 88. This is calculated by treating all Plain cards as being worth zero points (instead of 1), which reduces the total number of points in the deck to 240. Playing in this way does not change the gameplay significantly but speeds up the calculation of scores.[@IchinoseHanafuda 15]

### The Four-Three <span lang="ja-Latn">teyaku</span>

The Four-Three <span lang="ja-Latn">teyaku</span> is very rare (appearing in approximately 1 in 140 thousand hands, see below for precise calculations). Because of this, other optional rules for Four-Three have been developed.

For example:[@HachiHachi 34]
- instead of winning 20 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span>, active players must pay _all_ of their chips (except for debt chips) to the winner, and the campaign year (see below) is immediately ended
- players who are _not_ active must also pay half the value (10 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span>) to the winner

Or:[@Gimmi88]
- active players pay 30 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span>; inactive players pay 20 <span lang="ja-Latn">kan</span>

A reported superstition is that after Four-Three has been dealt, the deck should be burned.[@IchinoseHanafuda 43]

### The origin of <span lang="ja">赤短</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">aka-tan</span>

This <span lang="ja-Latn">yaku</span> is made up of exactly the three red <span lang="ja-Latn">tanzaku</span> cards of the first three months (pine, plum, cherry). No other <span lang="ja-Latn">tanzaku</span> may be used to complete it.

The cards are labelled <span lang="ja">𛀋らす</span> (<span lang="ja-Latn">urasu</span>), <span lang="ja">よろし</span> (<span lang="ja-Latn">yoroshi</span>), and <span lang="ja">す𛀙𛂦ら</span> (<span lang="ja-Latn">sugawara</span>).

It was also formerly known as <Pronounce lang="ja-Latn"  file="pronunciation_ja_裏菅原.mp3" pronouncer="mezashi">ura-sugawara</Pronounce> (<span lang="ja">裏菅原</span>, ‘behind/inside <span class="noun" lang="ja-Latn">Sugawara</span>’) or <span lang="ja-Latn">urasu</span> for short.[^fn10] This name was a reference to the popular <span lang="ja-Latn">kabuki</span> play <cite><Pronounce lang="ja-Latn"  file="pronunciation_ja_菅原伝授手習鑑.mp3" pronouncer="jinyamai">Sugawara Denju Tenarai Kagami</Pronounce></cite> (<span lang="ja">菅原伝授手習鑑</span>, ‘<cite>Sugawara and the Secrets of Calligraphy</cite>’). In the play the three main characters are brothers who are named after the three plants featured on the cards: <Pronounce noun="true" lang="ja-Latn"  pronouncer="mezashi" file="pronunciation_ja_松王丸.mp3">Matsuōmaru</Pronounce> (<span lang="ja">松王丸</span>, pine), <Pronounce noun="true" lang="ja-Latn"  pronouncer="mezashi" file="pronunciation_ja_梅王丸.mp3">Umeōmaru</Pronounce> (<span lang="ja">梅王丸</span>, plum), and <Pronounce noun="true" lang="ja-latn"  file="pronunciation_ja_桜丸.mp3" pronouncer="mezashi">Sakuramaru</Pronounce> (<span lang="ja">桜丸</span>, cherry). In the play the brothers wear costumes which bear symbols of the plants related to their names.

[^fn10]: Other spellings of <span lang="ja-Latn">urasu</span> (<span lang="ja">うらす</span>) include <span lang="ja">裏</span>, <span lang="ja">裏す</span>, <span lang="ja">宇良す</span>, or <span lang="ja">𛀋らす</span>.

The <span lang="ja-Latn">yaku</span> can also be called <span lang="ja-Latn">yoroshī</span> (<span lang="ja">よろしい</span>, ‘good’) or <span lang="ja-Latn">akayoro</span> (<span lang="ja">赤よろ</span>, ‘red good’), or even simply <span lang="ja-Latn">aka</span> (<span lang="ja">緋</span>, ‘scarlet’), although this risks confusion with the <span lang="ja-Latn">aka teyaku</span>.

## Equipment

Manufacturers used to produce small boxed sets of cards and implements to play the game with. These came in several forms, usually a rectangular box, but also in the form of a fake book which could be hidden amongst real books on a shelf.

### Sweets Cards · <span lang="ja">菓子札</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">kashifuda</span>

<span lang="ja-Latn">Kashifuda</span> are cards that are used to stand in for money, since playing gambling games for real money has almost always been illegal in Japan. The name means ‘sweets cards’, but is also a pun on <span lang="ja">貸し</span> <span lang="ja-Latn">kashi</span> (‘debt/favour’), so can be understood as ‘debt cards’. Thus, the name was originally probably meant to be the latter but was later modified into the current form. In any case, the cards usually follow along with this punning by displaying images of food and drink.

It is unclear how literally the images should be taken — did a card depicting a steak dinner require the loser to make payment in kind? It is fun to imagine so, but I have no evidence to support this.

## Sources & References

Books about <span lang="ja-Latn" class="noun">Hachi-Hachi</span> in English are rare. Coverage is also better in older books than newer, as several books were published in the early 20th century as an attempt to kickstart a craze for the game in the same way that [Mahjong](games/mahjong/mahjong.md) had taken off. The books of this era which I have referenced are @Gimmi88 and @HachiHachi, both of which were authored in English by Japanese players. More modern books tend to be translations or restatements of Japanese sources.

Books with English descriptions that are readily available are @Hanafuda [39] and @HanamiHanafuda [84].
